Introduction
The analysis of the birthweight dataset aims to provide valuable insights that can help predict and identify factors contributing to low birthweight, a critical factor in ensuring the health and well-being of newborns. By leveraging the provided data and applying various analytical techniques, this analysis seeks to uncover the relationships between maternal, paternal, and environmental characteristics and their influence on the baby's birthweight. The findings from this analysis can inform public health policies and interventions to promote healthier pregnancies and better birth outcomes. Additionally, elucidating these factors can lead to targeted interventions and personalized healthcare strategies, ultimately reducing the incidence of low birthweight and improving neonatal health globally.

def standard_scaler(df_full):
    """
    Standardizes a dataset (mean = 0, variance = 1). Returns a new DataFrame.
    Requires sklearn.preprocessing.StandardScaler()
    
    PARAMETERS
    ----------
    df_full     | DataFrame to be used for scaling
    """

    # INSTANTIATING a StandardScaler() object
    scaler = StandardScaler(copy=True)


    # FITTING the scaler with the data
    scaler.fit(df_full)


    # TRANSFORMING our data after fit
    x_scaled = scaler.transform(df_full)

    
    # converting scaled data into a DataFrame
    new_df = pd.DataFrame(x_scaled)


    # reattaching column names
    new_df.columns = list(df_full.columns)
    
    return new_df

Feature Engineering¶

In the Feature Engineering section, new features are created that could significantly impact the model's predictive capabilities.

Here is an analysis for each engineered feature:

  1. Age Difference Between Mother and Father (age_diff)

This feature captures the age gap between the mother and father. It's conceivable that the age difference may influence the health outcomes of the pregnancy, as significant age disparities could be associated with different lifestyle choices, socioeconomic status, or genetic diversity, all of which might impact the baby’s birthweight. By quantifying this gap, we can examine whether a larger age difference correlates with the risk of low birthweight, which could be important for targeted interventions.

  1. Education Difference Between Mother and Father (educ_diff)

Education level often correlates with health literacy, access to healthcare, and health behaviors, which can, in turn, influence pregnancy outcomes. By calculating the difference in education levels between parents, this feature aims to discern whether parental education parity plays a role in fetal development and birthweight. A significant difference might reflect an imbalance in knowledge or attitudes toward health-promoting behaviors, potentially affecting prenatal care and, consequently, the baby's birthweight.

  1. Total Number of Risk Factors (risk_factors)

This feature sums the number of cigarettes smoked (cigs) and instances of alcohol consumption (drink), providing a single, composite measure of maternal risk exposure. The combined effect of these risk factors could have a multiplicative rather than additive effect on the risk of low birthweight. This feature allows for the evaluation of cumulative lifestyle risks, considering that both smoking and alcohol use during pregnancy are well-documented risk factors for low birthweight.

  1. Racial Congruence Between Mother and Father (same_race)

This binary feature assesses whether the mother and father share the same racial background by comparing the binary race indicators (mwhte, mblck, moth, fwhte, fblck, foth). Racial congruence could be a proxy for genetic factors or might reflect cultural and socioeconomic factors that influence health outcomes, including access to care and health-related practices during pregnancy. Understanding if racial congruence correlates with birthweight may highlight the role of genetic compatibility or social determinants in birth outcomes.

  1. Ratio of Prenatal Visits to Mother's Age (visits_per_age)

This feature is created by dividing the number of prenatal visits by the mother’s age. Younger mothers might have different prenatal care needs or access compared to older mothers, and the frequency of visits relative to age could indicate the level of healthcare engagement or maternal health resource utilization. A higher ratio might suggest more intensive monitoring, possibly linked to higher-risk pregnancies, which could be inversely related to birthweight outcomes.

  1. Is there an official threshold that signifies when birthweight gets more dangerous? In other words, is there a cutoff point between a healthy birthweight and a non-healthy birthweight?

The threshold for low birth weight is less than 5 pounds, 8 ounces (2,500 grams) (Low Birth Weight, n.d.). Babies born below this weight are considered to have a low birth weight and may face various health challenges, though some can be healthy despite being small. Causes of low birth weight include premature birth, which is delivery before 37 weeks of pregnancy, and intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), which is poor growth within the womb due to placental, maternal, or fetal issues. Babies at risk for low birth weight include those born prematurely, with IUGR, or to mothers with certain health issues, such as infections, insufficient weight gain during pregnancy, a history of low-birth-weight babies, substance use, and mothers under 17 or over 35 years of age. Symptoms of low birth weight include small size and low body fat, and it is diagnosed through prenatal measurements and confirmed by weighing the baby after birth. Treatment often involves intensive care, temperature-controlled beds, and specialized feeding methods. The prognosis for low-birth-weight babies varies depending on the birth weight, with those weighing less than 500 grams facing the highest risks. Complications can range from trouble feeding and staying warm to more severe issues like respiratory distress and developmental delays.
